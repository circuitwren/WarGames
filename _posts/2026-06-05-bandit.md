The Bandit level of overthewire.org is a wargame designed to ease users into the world of BASH. Link: https://overthewire.org/wargames/bandit/bandit0.html 

OverTheWire is a free resource that my college introduced me to during my degree program. It's something I go back to when I need to retrain my basic BASH skills. This war game has taught me how to find files and search for patterns inside those files using the Bourne Again SHell (BASH), the successor to the UNIX shell designed in the 70's by Stephen Bourne.

Bourne created the UNIX Bourne Shell as a proprietary, closed-source OS for Bell Labs. However, we all stand on the backs of giants and Bourne was no exception. The Thompson Shell* was introduced by Ken Thompson before the Bourne Shell as a command interpreter to interact with the Unix system. The Bourne Shell was created in response to the Thompson Shell and introduced scripting capabilities on top of the command interpreter. It would be Seventh Edition Unix or the Bourne Shell that set the tone for modern Unix shell scripting. 

UNIX itself claims lineage rooted in MIT's Compatible Time-Sharing System (CTSS)**, which presented consoles for programmers to work directly with their computers for the first time. To put it in perspective, one of the authors of the CTSS paper, Fernando Jose Corbato (also known as the father of the computer password), won the 1990 Turing award for the core scheduling algorithm of the CTSS along with many other lifetime achievements that are too numerous to list here***.

Corbato represented a group of scientists and researchers dedicated to the open and free exchange of code operating under MIT's Project MAC. The cohort included but was not limited to Richard Greenblatt, Bill Gosper, Tom Knight, and later on Richard Stallman. The Project MAC group was a product of their time. The 60's and 70's counter cultures sprang up around and influenced them in ways that continue to influence modern computing today. Most importantly for this post is the concept of a free and open internet. Project MAC started by proposing free and open software before the internet or ARPAnet even existed and this same philosophy inspired the public internet today to remain public despite global attempts to lock that down****. ARPAnet may have been a government project, but it was run by a bunch of hippies. Richard Stallman and Fernando Jose Corbato would become famously involved in their disagreement over the use of passwords to protect systems. Stallman saw this as completely at odds with the culture that brought about software in the first place. Stallman sent a memo out to MIT staff encouraging everyone to abandon their password protocols altogether. The ultimate battle against a public good versus privacy and security is ongoing. It is a question we still haven't found one right answer for.

Shifting back to the modern day we have a free website called overthewire.org that teaches BASH. This website will teach anyone with an internet connection and computer to use BASH in an effort to promote security research. Much like Wikipedia it is kept up by staff volunteers and generous donations. Become a patron or donate here: https://overthewire.org/information/donate.html. Please note that I am not affiliated with the OverTheWire team in anyway and am not getting paid for this post. I see what this team has done for the rest of us in an effort to preserve the birthright of the internet itself, but freedom comes at a cost.

The Bandit level is like playing hopscotch. It is a web-based community learning center. Users can run through the puzzles and complete the game in 1 day. However, this isn't the best use of anyone's time. I found that completing a level and attempting to review it a couple days later without notes helped the lessons sink in deeper than surface level. I recommend this to anyone looking to "try before you buy" with the cyber security field. Especially if red-teaming is the end goal.
